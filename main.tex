% \begin{abstract}
There are several different approaches to causal inference. Two of the most popular are based on causal Bayesian networks and potential outcomes respectively. \citet{pearl_causality:_2009} has used a three-level hierarchy to discuss the relationship between these model types; causal Bayesian networks model ``see'' and ``do'' questions while potential outcomes model ``see'', ``do'' and ``imagine''. This paper clarifies the relationship between the model types, with care to distinguish formal differences between models from difference between the problems that are being modeled. We follow the tradition of the decision theoretic approach to causal inference by proposing a generic type of model appliccable to decision problems we call ``see-do models''. An important and difficult problem with see-do models is how to relate observations (``see'') to consequences (``do''). Causal Bayesian networks are a type of see-do model that propose that certain conditional probabilities are identical between observations and consequences. We show that potential outcomes arise in see-do models extended with \emph{functionally exchangeable subsequences}. It is possible to interpret these subsequences as additional propositions involving ``imagining'' rather than ``doing''
